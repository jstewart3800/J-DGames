Number 1 Pick

There's a reason why so many developers have marketted their games "as similar to the Souls-Borne series", or "Souls-like". Boasting an outstanding combat system, one-of-a-kind environmental storytelling, and outrageously difficult level design, Dark Souls is simultaneously one of the hardest and most rewarding games of all time. The soundtrack knows exactly where emphasis is needed and exactly how much; it remains minimalistic during normal exploration to provide a sense of unease and danger, provides a soothing backdrop when resting in Firelink Shrine, and gets the player's blood pumping during exhilarating boss fights. The true magic of Dark Souls lies in its co-op gameplay; in a world so dark and forboding, the comfort you feel knowing that there's just one person in the world who doesn't want you dead is immense. Some of my greatest friendships and most accomplished gaming moments have been solidified by the Souls series, and Dark Souls is the one that forever holds that special place in my heart.

Number 2 Pick

As an entry in the Halo series that is often overlooked, ODST arguably boasts the best story of them all. As the Rookie, You search through the ruins of New Mombasa to find your missing fireteam, wading through or sneaking past squads of Covenant scouring the ruins for survivors. Finding clues to their whereabouts along the way, each new mission reveals another ODSTs perspective, allowing the Rookie to reconstruct the story, link up with his squad and uncover the larger mystery of the city. The soundtrack sets exactly the right tone, from the adrenaline filled battle themes during the day, to the gorgeously melancholy jazz themes of the nighttime segments that really drives home that feeling of isolation and solitude. With the amazing voices of several talents from Firefly and delightful easter eggs, every second of this story remains one of my favorites to this day.

Number 3 Pick

Okami is by far the most visually beautiful game on this list. With heavy inspiration from the Legend of Zelda series and a japanese style of painting known as sumi-e, Okami's portrayal of a vibrant and colorful feudal Japan provides just the right mixture of gorgeous scenary, dangerous mythical beasts, tight mechanics, well-written characters, and all the puzzles and exploration anyone could ask for. The game's most famous feature, the celestial brush, allows the player to effect the world around them by simply drawing on it, making for an amazing tool and weapon in a world where exploration and efficient combat are well rewarded. With an awe-inspiring soundtrack that sets the tone just right and a story to match, Okami is always a good time.

Number 4 Pick

As a new Megaman coming to a new console, Mega Man X is one of the finest examples level and character development in gaming. From the moment the first stage begins, the game teaches you everything you need to know with nothing but the elements of the level itself; run to the right, shoot the things, jump occasionally, and most of all, don't get hit. With Vile beating you mericlessly and Zero subsequently returning the favor, you are immediately introduced to the idea that some day you too can be as powerful as they are, a message that is punctuated perfectly by the game's ending. Along with these marvels of game design, Mega Man X boasts incredibly smooth controls, stellar music, and beautiful level design that solidify its position as a pillar of gaming history.

Number 5 Pick

There are many entries in the Metroid series that could've made this list, but Zero Mission was my final choice for several reasons. Metroid: Zero Mission was my introduction to the series's classic 2D side-scrolling, upgrade-collecting, alien-blasting formula. As a remake of the 1986 original, Zero Mission combines all the exploration and mystery that put the series pregenitor on the map with the innovations and helpful features of the series's most popular title, Super Metroid, without compromising the series's staple feeling of growth and isolation. With all the glory of the original and some stellar additional content after the original ending, Zero Mission remains as much of a classic as the rest of the illustrious series.
